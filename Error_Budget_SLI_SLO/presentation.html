Defining Availability: SLIs

* Service Level Indicator

* This is a definition of what success looks like per transaction

* Usually includes a measurement of time, an availability goal and where the measurement is taken from

* Every SLI is a metric but NOT every metric is an SLI (or should be)

Freshness, correctness, throughput, availability, durability, quality, coverage, latency all make up reliability

* Durability - am I getting out the same thing I Put in?

* Quality - are you delivering part of or all of the experience?

* There are many ways to measure reliability but almost none of them are "up" or "down"

Our Sample Business

We have a pizza business that offers delivery. We have multiple locations in the city and we accept orders only from one of our online portals in able to stream line the process.

We are open 12PM - 12AM in 2 shifts: 12PM - 6PM and 6PM - 12AM

In a 6 hour window we are measuring the number of deliveries. In order to be successful the pizza must be delivered in less than 60 minutes as measured by the GPS in the delivery vehicle.

A successful shift is one that has a 95% success rate.

Some Common SLIs

Durability: Take particular care with durability SLIs: the data that the user wants may be only a small portion of the data that is stored.

Quality: For example, if the User Data store is unavailable, the game is still playable but uses generic imagery.

Some Common SLIs

Freshness: Ideally this metric counts how many times a user accessed the data, so that it most accurately reflects the user experience

Coverage: For batch processing, the proportion of jobs that processed above some target amount of data. For streaming processing, the proportion of incoming records that were successfully processed within some time window.

Maximum Effort Not Required!

We are shooting for just enough reliability.

Ideally we have the minimum user acceptance

Service Level Objectives

* Your SLOs are essentially a culmination of your SLIs in a given time frame

* In our simplified Pizza example our SLO is going to be the number of successful shifts / the total number of shifts. In a 1 month period this should be 98% of the shifts were marked as successful

* It's very important to set your SLOs appropriately as they make up the basis for your error budget

Users rarely experience the service the way you do

You have external dependencies that impact your SLOs

Budgeting: More Than Math

The primary function of the budget is to define direction

you need to set a plan and stick to it

It requires multiple layers of by in

Enforcement is required to be successful

Ultimately, setting a budget is about enumerating how much risk you can tolerate in order to make improvements

How Do You Determine How Much Risk?

Remember the users we defined earlier?

We want to make them happy

A User will never comment on a service being too reliable

You can optionally build "get out of jail free" cards into the risk profile

What To Do With Surplus?

* you need to think ahead

* Waste of errors to have it go to 'petty cash'

* Improve current services

* Risky experiments... how about injecting latency to see if user happiness drops?

* Investigate new technology

* Pay down tech debt

* successive under budget means you can consider reallocating resources

But What If You Fail?

* Halt changes

* Use your get-out-of-jail card

* Beef up instrumentation

* Slow your release cadence

* Adjust the SLI/SLOs... maybe they aren't appropriate

* Take no action and use it as a data point?

* In mature organizations they put a halt to deployments until the budget recovers

* Earlier we talked about budgeting for a get-out-of-jail free card... you could be forced to use one (or a few) of these to continue

* You could attempt to make non-impacting changes such as improving the alerting/metrics/paging/SOPs to hopefully reduce down time next budget cycle

* You can slow the release cadence to a trickle so that code authors are strongly incentivised to improve the stability of their releases

* If you have multiple budget failures it could be that your metrics are off base. It is an opportunity to review and revise

* There are a lot of things that can cause failure

* Every service in an app's path incurs risk

* If the service is outside of your org's control, you need to decide if this counted against you

Allocating For Boundaries

* If your org controls the services at the boundaries you need to account for this in your error budget

* If you have Kafka, SQL and a down stream API that are all required for your app, they need to be captured in your budget
